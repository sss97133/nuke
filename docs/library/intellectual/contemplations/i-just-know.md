# I Just Know

## The Epistemology of Expertise in the Physical-Asset Domain

---

> "The 'I just know' people... OK prove it. Show us your records. It will light the way."
> — Skylar, March 2026

---

## Abstract

In every market for physical assets — vehicles, artworks, watches, furniture, wine — there exists a class of individuals whose judgment is trusted despite their inability to fully articulate its basis. The dealer who can spot a repainted fender from across a parking lot. The connoisseur who can identify a Rembrandt school painting's actual author from the brushwork. The mechanic who can diagnose an engine problem from its sound. They "just know." This essay examines the epistemological status of this expertise — arguing that it is real, that it is valuable, that it is unauditable in its current form, and that the provenance engine's deepest purpose is not to replace it but to materialize it: to make the tacit explicit, the private public, the mortal portable. Show us your records. It will light the way.

---

## I. The Structure of Expert Judgment

When a seasoned dealer looks at a vehicle and says "that's a 50-thousand-dollar car," they are not consulting a pricing model. They are not running comparable sales through a spreadsheet. They are not applying a rubric with deductions for paint quality and additions for matching numbers. They are doing something at once simpler and more complex: they are comparing the vehicle against a mental library of every vehicle they have seen, bought, sold, and evaluated over decades of practice.

This mental library is vast, unstructured, and largely unconscious. It contains:

- **Thousands of visual impressions**: what factory paint looks like versus aftermarket paint, how original chrome ages differently from replated chrome, the specific way a door hangs when the hinges are worn, the color shift that occurs when a panel has been replaced and repainted.

- **Hundreds of market data points**: what a similar vehicle sold for last year, what the market is doing right now, how much premium matching numbers commands in this specific marque, how much discount a repaint carries in this specific community.

- **Dozens of contextual factors**: who the buyer pool is for this type of vehicle, what time of year it is, what the economic environment is, whether any comparable vehicles are currently on the market.

- **Pattern matches against failure cases**: the vehicle that looked perfect but had hidden frame rust, the restoration that used incorrect parts, the seller who described "numbers matching" when they meant "engine date code is close."

The dealer integrates all of this information in real time, producing a judgment that feels instantaneous and intuitive. They "just know." And they are usually right — not always, but significantly more often than a non-expert would be.

## II. Tacit Knowledge

The philosopher Michael Polanyi distinguished between explicit knowledge (knowledge that can be articulated and transmitted through language) and tacit knowledge (knowledge that is embedded in practice and cannot be fully articulated). His classic example: a person can ride a bicycle without being able to explain the physics of balance. The knowledge is in the body, not in the description.

Expert judgment in the physical-asset domain is substantially tacit. The dealer cannot fully explain how they know the paint is wrong. They can point to "something about the orange peel" or "the way it catches light," but these verbal descriptions are impoverished approximations of the actual perceptual discrimination they are performing. The knowledge is in the eye, trained by decades of looking. It cannot be transmitted through words alone.

This is not mysticism. Tacit knowledge is real knowledge — it produces reliable predictions and actionable judgments. But it has three critical limitations:

### Limitation 1: Non-Transferable

Tacit knowledge dies with its holder. When a legendary dealer retires, their accumulated expertise — the mental library of thousands of vehicles, the pattern recognition for condition assessment, the instinct for market timing — retires with them. Their successors must rebuild this library from scratch, through their own decades of experience.

This is an enormous waste. The dealer's expertise, accumulated over 40 years of daily practice, represents a dataset of extraordinary depth and specificity. It is arguably the most valuable dataset in their market segment. And it is stored in the most fragile and non-portable medium imaginable: a single human brain.

### Limitation 2: Non-Auditable

When the dealer says "that's a 50-thousand-dollar car," the client has two options: trust them or don't. There is no way to audit the judgment — to examine the evidence, assess the reasoning, identify potential blind spots, or calibrate the confidence level. The judgment is a black box.

This non-auditability creates a trust problem. The client must rely on the dealer's reputation, which is itself a form of social capital accumulated through years of reliable judgments. But reputation is a blunt instrument — it tells you the dealer has been right in the past, not that they are right now. And it is vulnerable to conflicts of interest: the dealer who is also the seller has an incentive to overvalue, and no mechanism for the buyer to verify.

### Limitation 3: Non-Scalable

Tacit expertise cannot be replicated. There can be only one of this dealer, one of this connoisseur, one of this mechanic. Their availability is limited by human constraints — geography, time, health, willingness to work. The market that depends on their judgment is bottlenecked at their capacity.

This creates structural inequality. Wealthy collectors who can afford to retain the top dealers have access to the best judgment. Everyone else must rely on their own inexpert assessment or on second-tier advisors. The market is efficient for the well-connected and opaque for everyone else.

## III. What "I Just Know" Actually Is

The claim "I just know" is not an appeal to mystery. It is a description of a specific cognitive process: unconscious pattern matching against a large, unstructured dataset of accumulated experience.

### The Cognitive Science

Expertise research, from de Groot's studies of chess masters to Kahneman and Klein's work on naturalistic decision-making, converges on a model: experts perceive patterns that novices cannot see. The chess master does not calculate more moves ahead than the novice — they recognize board positions from a library of tens of thousands of positions accumulated through years of play. The expert firefighter does not analyze the structural dynamics of a burning building — they recognize a pattern ("this feels like a backdraft scenario") from hundreds of previous fires.

The expert dealer does the same. They do not analyze the vehicle — they recognize it. The slightly wrong shade of red, the too-perfect chrome, the suspiciously even panel gaps — these are pattern violations that trigger recognition in the trained eye before conscious analysis can begin. The dealer's "I just know" is the verbal output of a pattern recognition system operating below the threshold of conscious articulation.

### The Data Beneath

But here is the critical insight: the patterns being matched are themselves derived from data. The dealer who recognizes wrong paint has seen hundreds of factory paint jobs and dozens of repaints. Each observation contributed to the mental model that now fires automatically. The dealer who senses overvaluation has tracked hundreds of sales and internalized the price distributions for this market segment. Each sale result refined the mental model.

"I just know" is the compressed output of a career's worth of observations. The knowledge is real. The observations that built it were real. The only problem is that those observations were never structured, never attributed, never stored in any system that could be audited, shared, or preserved.

## IV. The Provenance Engine as Knowledge Materializer

The provenance engine does not replace expert judgment. It materializes the observations that expert judgment is built from.

### Materializing Observations

When a dealer examines a vehicle, they make dozens of observations: the paint is consistent, the chrome is original, the interior is period-correct, the engine bay is clean, the frame shows appropriate patina. In current practice, these observations live in the dealer's head. They inform the dealer's judgment but are not captured in any external system.

The provenance engine provides a structure for capturing these observations. Each observation is an entry: "Dealer X observed that the paint appears to be factory original, based on examination of the doorjambs and underhood surfaces, on [date], at [location], with confidence level [high/medium/low]." The observation is attributed, timestamped, scoped, and stored.

One dealer's observations about one vehicle are moderately useful. But the system accumulates observations from many dealers about many vehicles over many years. The resulting dataset is not tacit knowledge — it is explicit, structured, auditable knowledge that approximates what the expert knows implicitly.

### From Individual Expertise to Collective Intelligence

The individual dealer knows 5,000 vehicles from personal experience. The system, after accumulating observations from hundreds of dealers, inspectors, auction houses, and enthusiasts, knows 500,000 vehicles from documented observation. The individual dealer's pattern recognition operates over their personal dataset. The system's pattern recognition can operate over a dataset that no individual could accumulate in a lifetime.

This does not make the system "smarter" than the dealer. The dealer's perceptual discrimination — the ability to see what the camera misses, to feel what the data cannot capture — remains uniquely human. But the system can identify patterns across the full dataset that no individual could see: market trends emerging across multiple auction houses, condition correlations between specific restoration shops and long-term durability, provenance patterns that predict authentication disputes.

The relationship is complementary: the dealer provides depth (rich, nuanced observations about specific vehicles), and the system provides breadth (pattern recognition across the full population of observed vehicles). Together, they know more than either alone.

### Making the Implicit Explicit

The most valuable function of the provenance engine is not replacing experts but making their knowledge legible. When an expert's observation is captured in the system, it becomes:

- **Auditable**: anyone can see what was observed, by whom, when, and on what basis
- **Challengeable**: another expert can record a contradicting observation, creating a dialogue that the system preserves
- **Persistent**: the observation survives the expert's retirement, illness, or death
- **Portable**: the observation is available to anyone with access to the system, not just to the expert's personal clients
- **Aggregable**: the observation combines with observations from other experts to produce composite assessments that exceed any individual's scope

This materialization is the fundamental value proposition of the system for expert users. The system is not saying "your expertise is wrong." It is saying "your expertise is too valuable to exist only in your head. Let us give it a body — a structured, persistent, shareable form that preserves your contributions for as long as the data exists."

## V. The Trust Problem

Expert judgment depends on trust. The client trusts the dealer. The buyer trusts the inspector. The collector trusts the curator. But trust is fragile, opaque, and concentrated.

### Fragile

Trust is built over years and destroyed in moments. A single bad recommendation, a single missed defect, a single conflict of interest exposed — and the expert's credibility collapses. The expert knows this, which makes them conservative: better to be cautious and maintain trust than to take a risk and lose it. This conservatism is rational for the individual expert but suboptimal for the market, because it means experts are systematically less willing to make bold calls that might be wrong.

### Opaque

Trust is a summary statistic. "I trust Dealer X" encodes years of interaction but provides no detail about what specifically makes Dealer X trustworthy. Does Dealer X have deep expertise in pre-war cars and shallow expertise in muscle cars? Is Dealer X reliable on condition assessments but unreliable on market valuations? The trust summary does not decompose into these dimensions.

The provenance engine decomposes trust into its components. A dealer's track record is not a single reputation score but a structured history: observations made, accuracy of those observations (as validated by subsequent evidence), domains of strength, domains of weakness, frequency and recency of contributions. The trust is still there — but it is legible.

### Concentrated

In most physical-asset markets, trust is concentrated among a small number of recognized experts. The auction house specialist. The marque club's technical advisor. The museum curator. The legendary dealer. Access to these experts is limited by geography, social connection, and economic status.

The provenance engine democratizes not the expertise itself — that remains the expert's contribution — but access to its outputs. When an expert's observation enters the system, it is available to anyone who queries that asset. The buyer in rural Montana has access to the same structured intelligence as the buyer in Manhattan. The college student researching a thesis has access to the same observation data as the billionaire collector.

This democratization does not diminish the expert. It amplifies them. The expert's observations, once captured, reach audiences they could never reach in person. Their influence extends beyond their personal network. Their legacy persists beyond their career.

## VI. The Tension

There is a genuine tension between tacit knowledge and structured data, and this essay does not pretend to resolve it.

### What Structure Captures

The provenance engine captures observations, attributions, timestamps, confidence levels, and source weights. It captures the explicit dimension of expertise: what was observed, by whom, when, and on what basis.

### What Structure Cannot Capture

The provenance engine cannot capture the perceptual discrimination that distinguishes the master from the competent practitioner. The way a trained eye reads brush strokes. The way a trained ear hears engine harmonics. The way a trained hand feels the difference between original plating and replating. These are embodied skills that resist articulation and resist capture.

The best condition assessment in the system — the most detailed, most attributed, most confident observation by the most trusted source — is still a verbal description of what the expert perceived. It is not the perception itself. The map is not the territory. The observation is not the expertise.

### Living with the Tension

The system acknowledges this tension by design:

1. **Human contributions are weighted highest among non-institutional sources.** A trusted human expert's observation outweighs an AI analysis, all else being equal. The system knows that human perception captures nuances that automated analysis misses.

2. **Contradictions are preserved, not resolved.** When two experts disagree, the system does not pick a winner. It presents both assessments with their respective trust weights and lets the consumer of the data decide. The resolution of expert disagreement requires further investigation — which the system can facilitate but not perform.

3. **The system invites correction.** Every AI-generated assessment, every automated classification, every computed composite is flagged as open to expert review. The expert can confirm, revise, or reject any automated output. The system learns from corrections over time.

4. **The goal is augmentation, not replacement.** The system does not claim to know what the expert knows. It claims to organize what experts have observed, to make their observations accessible, and to identify patterns across observations that no individual expert could see. The expert remains the primary sensor. The system is the memory, the aggregator, and the pattern detector.

## VII. Show Us Your Records

The injunction is not hostile. It is an invitation.

"Show us your records" says: your knowledge is valuable. So valuable that it should not exist only in your memory. Document what you see. Attribute what you claim. Date what you observe. Let the system hold your observations alongside observations from other experts, from institutions, from automated analysis, from the community at large.

"It will light the way" says: when all these observations are structured and connected, the picture that emerges is richer than any individual's knowledge. Not better — richer. The expert sees with unmatched depth in their domain. The system sees with unmatched breadth across all domains. Together, they illuminate what neither could see alone.

The dealer who "just knows" a car is worth $50,000 is right. The system that documents why — the comparable sales, the condition observations, the provenance chain, the market trajectory — does not invalidate the dealer's judgment. It validates it. It provides the evidence that the dealer's intuition compressed into a single number.

And in doing so, it makes the expertise accessible to everyone who lacks the dealer's decades of experience. The novice collector can now see what the expert sees — not the perceptual discrimination, which remains the expert's gift, but the structured evidence that informs the discrimination. The novice cannot spot a repaint by looking at it. But they can see in the system that three different experts have observed that the paint is factory original, based on doorjamb inspection, underhood inspection, and paint gauge measurement. The knowledge is materialized.

## VIII. The Mortality Problem

Experts die. This is the most urgent argument for knowledge materialization.

Every year, the physical-asset world loses experts whose accumulated knowledge has no successor. The 80-year-old dealer who knew every significant pre-war American car in the Northeast. The 70-year-old restorer who understood the factory paint processes of a specific manufacturer. The 90-year-old collector whose provenance files documented six decades of acquisitions across three continents.

When these people die, their knowledge dies with them. Not gradually — instantly. One day the expertise exists; the next day it does not. The mental library of 50,000 vehicle observations, the pattern recognition for authentication, the market intuition calibrated over half a century — all gone.

The provenance engine does not solve the mortality problem. No system can capture the full depth of a lifetime's expertise. But the system can capture the observations that the expert makes during their remaining years. Every observation entered into the system is an observation preserved. Every structured assessment is knowledge that will outlive its author.

The urgency is real. The experts who hold the deepest knowledge are the oldest. They are the least likely to adopt new technology. They are the most likely to dismiss structured data as inferior to their personal judgment (and they are not entirely wrong). But their personal judgment, unrecorded, dies with them. Their personal judgment, recorded in the system even partially, persists indefinitely.

The system's invitation to experts is not "we know better than you." It is "we need what you know. You are the primary source. Show us your records, however incomplete, however informal, however partial. Even a fraction of what you know, structured and preserved, is infinitely more than the nothing that survives your departure."

## IX. Toward a New Epistemology of Expertise

The provenance engine proposes a new relationship between expert judgment and structured data:

1. **Expert judgment is the primary sensor.** The human expert sees what cameras miss, knows what databases do not contain, and makes connections that algorithms cannot compute. The system does not replace this sensor — it records its outputs.

2. **Structured data is the shared memory.** What the expert observes enters the system as a structured observation, joining observations from all other sources. The collective memory exceeds any individual memory.

3. **The graph reveals patterns invisible to individuals.** Cross-referencing observations from hundreds of sources across thousands of assets over decades produces pattern insights that no individual expert could generate from their personal experience alone.

4. **Trust is earned and decomposed.** Expert authority in the system is not declared but accumulated through a track record of reliable observations. And it is not monolithic — the system knows which experts are strong in which domains.

5. **Knowledge is portable and persistent.** Observations entered into the system survive their author, travel beyond their author's personal network, and contribute to a composite understanding that grows with every addition.

This is not the replacement of expertise by data. It is the marriage of expertise and data — a partnership in which each contributes what the other lacks. The expert contributes depth, nuance, and perceptual discrimination. The system contributes breadth, persistence, and pattern detection. Together, they know the world of physical assets with a resolution that neither could achieve alone.

"I just know" remains true. It remains valuable. It remains human. The provenance engine simply asks: now show us. And together, we will light the way.

---

*This contemplation addresses the epistemological relationship between human expertise and structured data systems. It does not resolve the tension between tacit and explicit knowledge — that tension is productive and should be maintained. The practical implication is that the system must be designed to invite expert contribution, to weight it appropriately, and to preserve it indefinitely — not to replace it with automated alternatives.*
