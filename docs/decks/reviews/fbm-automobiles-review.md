# Fleet Luxe v9 — Review from FBM Automobiles

**Reviewer:** FBM Automobiles, Saint-Barthelemy
**Date:** 4 April 2026
**Document reviewed:** `docs/decks/fleet-luxe-v9.html`
**Perspective:** Operational partner — the one who has to make this work on the ground

---

## Summary

The deck is beautiful. The concept is sound — there is a real gap between what the hotels deliver and what the guest drives. We see it every day from across the runway. But this deck was written by someone who has never shipped a car to Saint-Barth. There are errors in the logistics, missing costs, no mention of FBM's compensation, and several assumptions about the regulatory environment that will not survive first contact with the Collectivite.

We can make this work. But not with what is in this deck. Below is what needs to change.

## 1. IMPORT PROCESS REALITY CHECK

### Shipping Route

The deck says "RORO from Savannah/Charleston to Gustavia." This is wrong. There is no RORO service to Gustavia. The port of Gustavia is a small harbor — it cannot receive RORO vessels. The actual route is:

1. **US East Coast port** (Jacksonville, Savannah, or Port Everglades are more common than Charleston for Caribbean shipping)
2. **Container or RORO to Pointe-a-Pitre, Guadeloupe** — this is the regional hub. CMA CGM, Marfret, and some smaller carriers serve this route.
3. **Barge from Guadeloupe to Gustavia** — vehicles are loaded onto a flat barge (Sodetra or similar). This is weather-dependent. During swells, the barge cannot dock. There is also a possible route through **Philipsburg, St. Maarten** and then barge to Gustavia, but Guadeloupe is more common for vehicles because customs clearance for French territory is simpler there.

Alternatively, some vehicles come via **St. Maarten** — shipped RORO to Philipsburg, then barged across. This route is faster but creates a customs complication: the vehicle enters Dutch territory first, then must clear French customs on arrival in SBH. We have done both. Guadeloupe is cleaner on paper.

**Container vs RORO:** For four vehicles, a 40-foot container holds two Broncos (maybe — the Bronco is wide). You would need two containers or use RORO to Guadeloupe and barge from there. RORO to Guadeloupe is available but less frequent than container. For four units, I would recommend **two 40-foot containers to Pointe-a-Pitre, then barge to Gustavia.** Budget accordingly — containers are more expensive than the $3,000/unit shown in the deck.

### Shipping Cost

$3,000 per vehicle is low. Realistic cost for the full chain:

| Segment | Per Vehicle |
|---------|-----------|
| US port to Guadeloupe (container, share of 40ft) | $2,500 - $3,500 |
| Guadeloupe port handling + customs transit | $400 - $600 |
| Barge Guadeloupe to Gustavia | $800 - $1,500 |
| Gustavia port handling | $200 - $400 |
| **Total shipping** | **$4,000 - $6,000** |

At $3,000 the deck is $1,000 - $3,000 short per unit. For four vehicles, that is $4,000 - $12,000 missing from the budget.

### Customs — Octroi de Mer

The 8% octroi de mer is approximately correct for vehicles, but the deck omits:

- **Droit de quai (dock tax):** 2.5% on CIF value. This applies to all imports into SBH.
- **TVA (VAT):** SBH is a free port with no TVA on most goods, BUT vehicles may trigger special treatment depending on classification. Currently vehicles are exempt from TVA in SBH, but this should be confirmed with the Collectivite for fleet/commercial use vehicles.
- **Customs broker fees:** $300 - $500 per vehicle for the declaration processing.

So the actual import tax burden is closer to **10.5% of CIF value** (8% octroi + 2.5% droit de quai), not 8%.

On a $59,500 vehicle with $5,000 shipping:
- CIF value: ~$64,500
- Octroi de mer (8%): $5,160
- Droit de quai (2.5%): $1,612
- **Total duties: ~$6,772** per vehicle, not $5,000

### Timeline

The deck does not state a timeline from commitment to vehicles-on-ground. This is a serious omission. Realistic timeline:

| Step | Duration |
|------|----------|
| Order/locate vehicles at US dealers | 1 - 3 weeks |
| Export paperwork (MSO, EEI/AES, customs broker) | 1 - 2 weeks |
| Transport to US port | 1 week |
| Sailing to Guadeloupe | 10 - 14 days |
| Guadeloupe port clearance + barge scheduling | 3 - 10 days |
| Barge to Gustavia | 1 day (weather permitting) |
| Gustavia customs clearance | 2 - 5 business days |
| Carte grise + insurance | 1 - 3 weeks |
| PDI, detail, prep | 2 - 3 days |
| **Total** | **8 - 12 weeks minimum** |

If the deck targets vehicles on-ground by October for high season, the order must be placed by **July at the latest.** During hurricane season (June - November), barge schedules become unreliable. This is not mentioned anywhere in the deck.

### MSO Process

The MSO (Manufacturer's Statement of Origin) approach is correct for new, untitled vehicles. However, the deck should clarify: the MSO must be endorsed by the selling dealer and accompany the vehicle through export. If the vehicle is titled in any US state first — even briefly — the MSO is void and you need a title, which complicates the SBH registration process. This must be done right the first time.

## 2. REGISTRATION & COMPLIANCE

### Carte Grise

The carte grise process in SBH is handled through the Prefecture (Collectivite de Saint-Barthelemy, not the standard French prefectoral system since the 2007 status change). It is slower and more opaque than mainland France.

Requirements:
- Proof of customs clearance (octroi de mer receipt)
- Certificate of conformity (or attestation from manufacturer — since these are US-spec vehicles, a European Certificate of Conformity does not exist. We will need either a Ford attestation or a DREAL dispensation)
- Proof of insurance
- Proof of identity and address for the registered owner
- If registered to a company: company registration documents (Kbis or SBH equivalent)

**The conformity issue is the biggest risk the deck does not address.** US-spec vehicles do not have European type approval. SBH follows French vehicle regulations in principle. For a one-off import, the Collectivite has been flexible — we have registered US vehicles before. For a fleet of four identical vehicles presented as a commercial operation, they may require a formal homologation or at minimum a manufacturer attestation that the vehicle meets basic safety standards. This needs to be sorted **before** the vehicles ship, not after.

**Timeline:** 1 - 3 weeks after all documents are submitted. But assembling the documents can take longer if the conformity question is not pre-arranged.

**Cost:** Registration fees in SBH are modest — approximately 200 - 500 EUR per vehicle depending on fiscal horsepower rating. The Bronco 2.7L V6 will be rated high on the French fiscal HP scale (likely 15-20 CV fiscaux), which pushes toward the higher end.

### Controle Technique

The deck says "new vehicles typically exempt initially." This is correct for vehicles under 4 years old registered in France/SBH — they do not need a controle technique at first registration. After that, the first inspection is at 4 years, then every 2 years. However, as US-spec vehicles without European type approval, the Collectivite **may** require an initial inspection as a condition of registration. We have seen this both ways. It should not be assumed as exempt — budget for it.

### Insurance

The deck says "FBM sources through local insurer" but gives no cost estimate. This is a significant omission.

Fleet insurance for 4 luxury SUVs used by hotel guests (i.e., multiple drivers, tourists unfamiliar with island roads, potentially intoxicated, on steep narrow roads with no guardrails):

- **Insurer options on SBH:** GAN, Allianz (via local agent), and a few local brokers. The market is small.
- **Risk profile:** Fleet vehicles driven by rotating hotel guests are HIGH risk. This is not a personal vehicle policy. This is a commercial fleet/rental policy.
- **Estimated annual premium per vehicle:** 4,000 - 7,000 EUR. Could be higher if the insurer classifies this as a rental operation (which it functionally is).
- **For 4 vehicles:** 16,000 - 28,000 EUR per year (~$17,000 - $30,000).
- **Excess (deductible) per incident:** Likely 1,000 - 2,000 EUR minimum. Could be higher for inexperienced or tourist drivers.

The deck must include this as a line item. It is a major recurring cost.

### Vehicle Size Restrictions

The Bronco 4-door is approximately 1,930 mm wide (mirrors folded) and 2,180 mm with mirrors. Some roads in SBH are very narrow — Lurin hill, the road to Colombier, the one-lane sections around Corossol. The Bronco will fit, but it is not comfortable. Guests will scratch it. This is not a theoretical risk — it is a certainty.

The bigger concern is **parking in Gustavia.** Parking spaces are sized for European vehicles. A Bronco will not fit in a standard Gustavia parking space. Guests will be frustrated.

The Bronco is a good choice for the brand image but a challenging choice for the island's infrastructure. The deck should acknowledge this and explain how guest orientation will address it (drive routes, parking guidance, etc.).

## 3. MAINTENANCE & OPERATIONS

### Salt Air Corrosion

This is the number one issue the deck ignores. Saint-Barthelemy is 25 square kilometers surrounded by ocean. Salt air is constant. Every vehicle on this island corrodes. A $60,000 vehicle will show corrosion on exposed metal, electrical connectors, brake components, and underbody within **6 months** without aggressive preventive maintenance.

Required maintenance cadence for a fleet vehicle in SBH salt air:
- **Underbody wash and inspection:** Every 2 weeks
- **Brake inspection:** Monthly (salt destroys rotors and calipers rapidly)
- **Electrical connector inspection and dielectric grease:** Quarterly
- **Full detail including underbody coating refresh:** Every 3 months
- **Ceramic coating or equivalent paint protection:** Applied at delivery, refreshed annually

Annual cost for aggressive corrosion prevention per vehicle: **$2,000 - $4,000.**

Without this, the vehicles will look terrible within a year. For a luxury placement, this is non-negotiable.

### Spare Parts

Ford has no dealer network in the French Antilles for the Bronco. SOGUAVA distributes some Ford commercial vehicles but not the current Bronco. Parts must come from:

1. **US mainland** — via air freight (DHL/FedEx to SXM, then onward to SBH) or by sea
2. **Lead time:** Air freight: 5 - 10 business days. Sea freight: 3 - 6 weeks.
3. **Cost:** Air freight for a fender or bumper cover: $200 - $500 shipping alone.

For consumables (filters, brake pads, fluids), we would stock these at FBM. For body panels, bumpers, glass — these come from the US and take time.

**Critical: if a vehicle is off the road waiting for parts, it is off the fleet.** With only 4 vehicles, losing one for 2 weeks is a 25% capacity reduction. The deck needs a **parts float** budget — pre-positioned common wear items and body panels.

Recommended pre-positioned stock per fleet:
- 2 sets of brake pads + rotors (front and rear)
- 1 set of wiper blades
- Cabin and engine air filters (4 sets)
- Coolant, brake fluid, AC refrigerant
- 1 replacement headlight assembly
- 1 windshield (guests will crack these on the hill roads)
- Touch-up paint in both colors

Estimated pre-positioned parts inventory: **$3,000 - $5,000.**

### Tires

The 2026 Bronco Heritage Edition runs 285/70R17 tires (33-inch). These are not stocked on SBH. They are not common in Guadeloupe either. They must be ordered from the US or from European off-road tire distributors.

Lead time: 2 - 4 weeks. We can stock 1 - 2 spares per size at FBM, but they take up space.

The island is hard on tires — sharp turns, rough roads, occasional off-pavement. Budget **one full set per vehicle per year** ($1,200 - $1,600 per set).

### AC System

In Caribbean heat and humidity, the AC runs at maximum capacity every day. It is the single most stressed system on the vehicle. Maintenance frequency:

- **Cabin filter replacement:** Every 3 months (salt air + humidity = mold)
- **Refrigerant check:** Every 6 months
- **Condenser cleaning:** Every 3 months (salt buildup reduces efficiency)
- **Full AC service (evacuate + recharge):** Annually

If the AC fails, the vehicle is effectively unusable. Guest will refuse it. Budget for rapid AC repair capability.

### Detailing Between Guests

Assuming the Bronco is used for guest stays averaging 5 - 7 nights:

- **Full detail between guests:** Interior vacuum, wipe-down, exterior wash, glass, tire dress. ~2 hours per vehicle.
- **Cost:** If FBM handles this, approximately 150 - 250 EUR per detail.
- **Frequency:** Potentially 20 - 30 times per vehicle per high season (Nov - Apr).
- **Season cost per vehicle:** 3,000 - 7,500 EUR.
- **For 4 vehicles:** 12,000 - 30,000 EUR per season.

Who handles this? FBM can, but this is a significant labor commitment on top of our existing business. This needs to be clearly specified in the operational agreement.

### Storage

Four Broncos take up space. When not in use (off-season: May - October, plus gaps between guests):

- **FBM lot:** We have limited space. Our lot at the airport serves our existing showroom and service clients. We can accommodate 2 Broncos on the lot. Not 4.
- **Alternative:** A dedicated parking area is needed. The hotel may have parking, but hotel parking in Gustavia is scarce and expensive.
- **Covered storage is essential** for corrosion reasons — salt air is worse for parked vehicles than driven ones (no airflow, moisture sits).

Storage cost if rented: 300 - 500 EUR/month per vehicle for covered parking on SBH. That is 14,400 - 24,000 EUR per year for 4 vehicles.

### Landed Cost

| Export filing | $200 - $400 |

The deck is **$4,500 - $7,400 short per vehicle.** For four vehicles, the total is closer to **$290,000 - $302,000**, not $273,000.

At invoice pricing ($55,000/unit), the total would be approximately $265,000 - $278,000.

### FBM's Compensation

The deck describes FBM as "the operational backbone" but mentions no compensation structure whatsoever. FBM is expected to:

- Handle import logistics
- Process customs and registration
- Source insurance
- Maintain 4 vehicles year-round
- Detail between guests
- Provide storage
- Be the on-call support for guest vehicle issues
- Sell the vehicle to the hotel (with what margin?)

**What FBM needs:**

1. **Dealer margin on the sale:** 8 - 12% is standard for an island import with full handling. On a $72K vehicle, that is $5,800 - $8,600 per unit. This is how FBM earns on the initial transaction.
2. **Monthly fleet management retainer:** 1,500 - 2,500 EUR per vehicle per month. This covers scheduling, coordination, basic oversight, storage allocation, insurance administration.
3. **Per-service billing:** Maintenance, detailing, and repairs billed at FBM's shop rate (currently 65 - 85 EUR/hour depending on work type).
4. **Emergency call-out fee:** If a guest calls at 10pm with a flat tire, someone from FBM responds. This should be a per-incident fee (200 - 400 EUR) or built into the retainer.

None of this is in the deck. Without a clear compensation structure, FBM has no reason to prioritize this over our existing business.

### Bodywork After Guest Incidents

Guests will hit things. It happens to every rental/fleet vehicle on SBH. The narrow roads, steep hills, and stone walls guarantee it.

- **Minor scrape/touch-up:** 1 - 2 days, 300 - 800 EUR
- **Panel repair and repaint:** 3 - 7 days, 1,000 - 3,000 EUR
- **Major bodywork (fender replacement, structural):** 2 - 4 weeks (waiting for parts from US), 3,000 - 8,000 EUR

During repair, the vehicle is off the fleet. With 4 vehicles, this is a capacity problem.

## 5. THE SOGUAVA PROBLEM

SOGUAVA (Societe Guadeloupeenne de l'Automobile) distributes Ford commercial vehicles in the French Antilles. They also distribute Suzuki, which is why every rental counter on SBH has Jimnys. The deck correctly identifies them as a potential bottleneck.

**The real situation:**

- SOGUAVA has a distribution agreement with Ford for the French Antilles territory. The scope of this agreement (whether it covers all Ford models or only commercial vehicles) is not publicly known.
- If SOGUAVA has territorial exclusivity for Ford passenger vehicles, importing Broncos directly from the US may violate that agreement. Ford's brand team would need to either (a) confirm SOGUAVA's agreement does not cover the Bronco, or (b) instruct SOGUAVA to cooperate, or (c) grant a specific exemption for this activation.
- **FBM has imported US-market vehicles before.** We have brought in Jeeps, pickups, and classic vehicles from the US. These were typically brands without a local distributor, or used vehicles that fall outside distribution agreements. A new Ford from a US dealer is different — it is within SOGUAVA's potential territory.

**Workaround options:**

1. **Ford brand team instructs SOGUAVA** to facilitate or stand aside. This requires Ford corporate involvement.
2. **Purchase through SOGUAVA.** They mark up the vehicle significantly and the lead time is worse. But it eliminates the legal question.
3. **Register the vehicles as personal imports** (import individuel). This is possible but slower and may require DREAL homologation.
4. **The VLVA intermediary path** described in the deck may work for the US side, but does not solve the French Antilles distribution question on the SBH side.

The deck needs to resolve the SOGUAVA question **before** spending money on vehicles. If SOGUAVA blocks this and Ford does not intervene, the entire plan stops.

## 6. STAFFING & CAPACITY

### Current FBM Operations

FBM runs a full-service garage and dealership. We service every brand on the island. We do bodywork, painting, mechanical repair, diagnostics, AC work, and sales. Our staff is sized for this workload. Adding fleet management for 4 luxury vehicles with guest-facing service expectations is a real commitment.

### What This Requires

- **Dedicated fleet coordinator:** At minimum a half-time person to handle scheduling, guest handoff, condition checks, detailing coordination, and insurance claims. On SBH, hiring is difficult and expensive. Budget 2,500 - 3,500 EUR/month for this role.
- **Additional detailing capacity:** Either a dedicated detailer or outsourced to a local detailing service. Our mechanics should not be pulled off repair work to wash Broncos.
- **Parts management:** Someone must track inventory, place orders, track shipments. This is administrative work.

### 24/7 Support

The deck implies FBM will be available for guest issues at any time. Fouquet's guests expect immediate response. If a Bronco has a flat tire at 10pm, if the AC stops working on a Saturday, if a guest locks the keys inside — who responds?

FBM's garage closes in the evening. We do not currently offer 24/7 service. To provide this:

- An on-call rotation would be needed (FBM staff or subcontracted)
- On-call pay: 500 - 1,000 EUR/month per person on rotation
- Emergency response: billed per incident

This must be explicitly defined in the operating agreement. "FBM handles it" is not a staffing plan.

### Willingness

FBM is interested in this project. The concept is good and the brand visibility is valuable — our airport-front location makes us part of the story. But interest is not commitment. Commitment requires:

1. A signed operating agreement with defined scope, compensation, and liability
2. A realistic budget that covers FBM's actual costs
3. Pre-funded parts inventory
4. Clear liability boundaries
5. A timeline that respects island logistics

## 7. WHAT FBM NEEDS IN THE NEXT VERSION

### 7.1 Operational Agreement Outline

The deck must include at minimum a term sheet or operational framework:
- FBM's role: defined scope of services
- FBM's compensation: dealer margin on sale, monthly retainer, hourly rate for service
- Performance standards: response times, turnaround times, reporting
- Term: minimum commitment period (suggest 2 years to justify staffing investment)

### 7.2 Realistic Timeline

A Gantt-style timeline from decision to vehicles-on-ground:
- Week 0: Sign operational agreement
- Week 1-3: Resolve SOGUAVA question, source vehicles
- Week 3-5: Export paperwork, transport to port
- Week 5-8: Ocean transit to Guadeloupe
- Week 8-10: Barge to SBH, customs clearance
- Week 10-12: Registration, insurance, PDI, ceramic coating, detail
- Week 12: Vehicles ready for placement

### 7.3 FBM Compensation Structure

Explicitly stated in the deck:
- **Sale margin:** X% on landed cost
- **Monthly retainer:** X EUR per vehicle
- **Service labor:** FBM shop rate
- **Emergency on-call:** X EUR per incident
- **Annual review:** compensation adjusted based on fleet size and utilization

### 7.4 Parts and Maintenance Budget

A dedicated annual budget for:
- Pre-positioned parts inventory (initial: $3,000 - $5,000)
- Scheduled maintenance per vehicle per year
- Unplanned repairs reserve ($5,000 - $10,000 per year)
- Tire replacement
- Corrosion prevention program

### 7.5 Liability Boundaries

This is critical and entirely absent from the deck:
- **Guest damages vehicle:** Who pays the deductible? Fouquet's? The guest? FBM should not carry this risk.
- **Vehicle damages guest property or third party:** Insurance covers it, but who manages the claim?
- **Mechanical failure injures guest:** Product liability. FBM as service provider has exposure here. This must be addressed.
- **Vehicle stolen:** It has happened on SBH. Who bears the loss?
- **FBM negligence in maintenance causes failure:** FBM carries professional liability insurance, but limits must be defined.

### 7.6 Corrected Economics Slide

The economics slide (slide 11) must be revised with:
- Corrected shipping costs ($4,500 - $6,000, not $3,000)
- Droit de quai (2.5%) added
- Customs broker fees added
- Pre-delivery prep costs added
- Annual operating cost summary
- FBM compensation included

### 7.7 Off-Season Plan

The deck focuses on high season. What happens May through October?
- Vehicles sit in covered storage (cost?)
- Reduced maintenance cadence but corrosion prevention continues
- Insurance continues (or can policies be suspended? Unlikely for fleet.)
- Hurricane preparedness: where are vehicles stored during a Cat 4?

## BOTTOM LINE

The concept is good. The gap is real. Ford and Fouquet's together on SBH is a strong story. FBM wants to be part of it.

But this deck is a vision document, not an operations plan. To turn it into something FBM can sign, it needs:

1. **Corrected logistics** — no RORO to Gustavia, add the barge leg, fix the costs
2. **SOGUAVA resolved** — this is a go/no-go gate
3. **FBM compensation defined** — we are a business, not a volunteer service
4. **Annual operating budget** — $150K - $270K/year for 4 vehicles is realistic
5. **Liability framework** — who pays when things break
6. **Conformity/homologation** — US-spec vehicles in a French jurisdiction need paperwork
7. **Timeline** — 12 weeks minimum, start by July for October delivery

We have been on this island since 1994. We have imported hundreds of vehicles. We know what works and what does not. This can work — but only if the next version of this deck respects the reality of getting a vehicle from Michigan to a 25-square-kilometer island in the French Caribbean.
